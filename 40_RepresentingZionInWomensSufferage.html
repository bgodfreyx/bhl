Deeper Dive: Representing Zion in Women’s Sufferage

In early 1879, 28-year-old Zina Prescindia ventured farther from home than she had gone before. She and Emmeline B. Wells served as delegates to the 11th National Woman Suffrage Convention in Washington, D.C. While there, Zina Prescindia wrote to her sister Susa about her experience; you can see a copy of her letter on the desk in this room. Among other highlights, they visited the White House and met United States President Rutherford B. Hayes and his wife, Lucy.

Zina Prescindia and Emmeline’s participation in the convention was bigger than politics. They sought to expand opportunities for women and to show the nation that Latter-day Saint women were thoughtful and capable—not the powerless, voiceless figures critics imagined. Their leadership became another way of building Zion: lifting women, defending their community, and shaping the future.

“Riggs House,” Washington, January 16th, 1879

My darling sister Susa,

Are you thinking of me tonight? My heart is going out to you. Shall I tell of my life as I am now for a few more days? The strains of lovely music come to me from the grand dining hall below as the band always plays at dinner, such ravishing music. I have just dined at 6 P.M. Everything is in grand style. We have a cozy room with everything at our command. Just now my heart is in Provo, by you with my darling children in my arms, my precious mother’s hand in mine, and looking in your eyes, seeing such a world of love in your face. Now to tell of some things we have done. Aunt Em and myself have been twice to the White House, have met Mrs. Hayes and Mr. Hayes. Were listened to with attention and respect. Mrs. Hayes took us round the house into her bedrooms and in the conservatory, the lovely flowers. Oh! How I wish you were here to see and hear all told! We have been round the Capitol. I will tell you all about it when I see you. To the Theater (National). To the art gallery, filled with statues, and pictures, curiosities, and “virtue.” The convention was reported in the papers. I sent copies to J.C.Y. [James C. Young] of the “Enquirer.”

Aunt Em’s health is not as good as it might be. She sends love to you.

Now my dear sis, you must give my best love to Brother Maeser and all the teachers and scholars in the school for me. Tell them to be happy. The world can never, no never give them the great happiness they enjoy. How my soul longs for the society of Saints. I feel, through the blessing of God and the faith of the Saints, we have been able to do some good. The future must tell how much.

Give my love to my “patients” and his patient wife, to all enquiring friends not forgetting Lyman, Teenie & Zina, and my girls. Remember them all. Sue dear, try to help my little son Sterling. Love him and think how lonesome he must be and do all for him you can. When you go west, I may be able to return the compliment.

We shall start for home sometime the fore part of next week. You ought to see the style I put on, but, Sue, I am myself all the same. A Mrs. Spencer is more than kind to us. Well, goodnight. My love to all. Don’t forget me in your prayers. And remember me to Susa my own darling sister. God bless you and yours.

As ever,

Zina
